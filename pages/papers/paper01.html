Neural Architecture Search for Efficient Models

Dr. Emily Zhang
• Published: June 15, 2023 • Last updated: July 2, 2023

Abstract

We present a novel approach to neural architecture search (NAS) that reduces computational requirements by 40% while maintaining accuracy. Our method combines gradient-based architecture optimization with a novel efficiency predictor that guides the search toward more computationally efficient architectures. We evaluate our approach on multiple benchmarks, including ImageNet and CIFAR-100, demonstrating state-of-the-art results with significantly reduced search costs. The resulting architectures achieve comparable accuracy to manually designed networks while requiring fewer parameters and FLOPs, making them particularly suitable for edge devices and real-time applications.

Introduction

Neural Architecture Search (NAS) has emerged as a powerful technique for automating the design of neural network architectures. While early NAS methods produced impressive results, they often required enormous computational resources, making them impractical for widespread use. Recent work has focused on making NAS more efficient, but there remains a significant gap between the computational cost of NAS and manual architecture design.

Related Work

Previous approaches to efficient NAS can be broadly categorized into three groups: weight-sharing methods, performance prediction methods, and gradient-based optimization. DARTS introduced differentiable architecture search, while ProxylessNAS demonstrated the effectiveness of path-level binarization. Our work builds upon these approaches but introduces a novel efficiency predictor that directly optimizes for computational efficiency during the architecture search process.

Methodology

Our method consists of three key components: (1) a differentiable architecture search space that includes both macro and micro architecture decisions, (2) an efficiency predictor that estimates the computational cost of candidate architectures, and (3) a multi-objective optimization procedure that balances accuracy and efficiency.

Results

We evaluate our approach on three standard benchmarks: CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet. On CIFAR-100, our method discovers architectures that achieve 96.2% accuracy with only 2.3M parameters, compared to 95.8% accuracy with 3.7M parameters for the best previous NAS method. The total search cost is reduced from 12 GPU days to 7 GPU days, a 42% reduction.

Conclusion

We have presented an efficient neural architecture search method that significantly reduces computational requirements while discovering high-performance architectures. Our results suggest that direct optimization for efficiency during the search process can lead to better architectures than post-hoc pruning or quantization of architectures designed purely for accuracy. Future work will explore extending this approach to additional architecture types and hardware platforms.
